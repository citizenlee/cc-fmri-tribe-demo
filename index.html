Headline finding

Hard to read ≠ hard to do. The two protocols land on opposite sides of the diagonal — watching plastic recruits more cognitive control, reading spore recruits more.

The single number on each axis is the cognitive-demand score = engagement in association-cortex networks (Frontoparietal Control + Dorsal Attention + Salience/Ventral Attention) divided by engagement in primary-sensory networks (Visual + SomatoMotor). A higher number means more of the brain's "thinking" machinery is recruited relative to its "perceiving" machinery.

  • Plastic sits above the diagonal — watching the video recruits more cognitive control than reading the description. The Cricut tool and its software workflow are unfamiliar; the viewer's brain has to track novel procedures step-by-step.
  • Spore sits below the diagonal — reading the description is harder than watching. The protocol has more abstract terminology (biosafety, miracloth filtration, conidiophores, etc.) and longer step chains that demand integration during comprehension.

Per-network breakdown

Which of the seven Yeo cortical networks does each protocol engage? Each axis is normalized to the max between the two protocols, so the radar shape shows relative engagement on each network.

What jumps out

  • Frontoparietal Control: plastic dominates (1.38× spore) — driving the higher "watch" cog-demand score
  • Occipital (Visual): spore dominates (1.05× plastic) — the spore video has more visually rich, fine-detail content
  • Sensorimotor and DorsAttn are nearly tied across protocols

Modality attribution

For each protocol we ran TRIBE four times — once with all modalities, then with each of video / audio / text zeroed in turn. The variance of (full − no_X) per vertex is how much modality X contributed to the prediction.

Text dominates by ~7× over video and ~20× over audio in both protocols. That says more about TRIBE than about brains: its Llama-3.2-3B text encoder produces denser, faster-varying features than its video/audio encoders, so the fusion transformer leans on it. It does not mean a real viewer's brain pays 7× more attention to narration.

Caveats

Watching ≠ doing. TRIBE predicts the brain of a passive observer. A real practitioner's brain engages motor cortex and somatosensory feedback far more.
N = 2. Two protocols is anecdote, not statistics. To claim "spore is harder than plastic" with confidence you'd want 5–10 protocols of clearly varying complexity.
Step timestamps are approximate. Evenly distributed across video duration — accurate within ~20s per step but not exact.
Modality attribution is model-side. Reflects TRIBE's learned priors, not how a real brain weights modalities.

What we built

  1. Inference pipeline on Modal — wrapped TRIBE v2 in a Modal app (tribe_modal.py): pulls weights from HuggingFace (facebook/tribev2 + the gated meta-llama/Llama-3.2-3B), runs whisper → Llama → multimodal fusion, returns per-vertex predicted BOLD on the fsaverage5 cortical mesh.
  2. Four runs per protocol:
    • Full V+A inference on the protocol video
    • Text-only inference on the Prism protocol description (gTTS → whisper → Llama → predict)
    • Three modality ablations (V / A / T each zeroed) for per-vertex attribution
  3. Analysis layer — Yeo-7 network parcellation, Destrieux atlas hover labels, cognitive-demand score, modality attribution maps.
  4. Three browser viewers — static contrasts, two animated protocol timelapses with rotatable brain, synced video, network timeseries, narration density, and protocol-step list.
